The Evolution of the Web

The World Wide Web has been around for more than 25 years, during which it has undergone significant transformations. Initially, it was met with euphoric enthusiasm, leading to rapid and widespread adoption. This was followed by the infamous "dot-com bust," a period characterized by economic downturns that forced many companies to close their doors. However, the web didn't just survive this phase; it experienced an innovation-driven rebirth that set the stage for the continuous evolution we see today. What started as a simple platform for sharing documents has become an indispensable communication and commercial medium that is deeply integrated into our daily lives. From desktops to smartphones, tablets, TVs, and even emerging devices like smartwatches and voice assistants, the web's presence is pervasive, and its influence is ever-expanding. As a result, there has never been a better time to leverage web design and development skills in a world that increasingly demands them.

Getting Started in Web Design

Through my extensive experience teaching web design courses and workshops, I've had the privilege of meeting a diverse array of individuals who are eager to learn how to build web pages. Their backgrounds vary widely, but their enthusiasm for learning is a common thread that unites them. Here are just a few examples:

  • The Experienced Print Designer: "I've been a print designer for 17 years, and now I'm feeling the pressure to provide web design services. The industry is shifting, and I need to adapt my skills to stay relevant."
  • The Seasoned Programmer: "I've been a programmer for years, but I want to shift my skills to web development because there are lucrative job opportunities in my area. The web is where the action is now."
  • The Enthusiastic Hobbyist: "I tinkered with web pages back in high school, and I think it might be something I'd like to do for a living. I want to turn my hobby into a career."
  • The WordPress User: "I've made a few sites using themes in WordPress, but I'd like to expand my skills and create custom sites for small businesses. I want to offer more personalized and sophisticated services to my clients."

Despite their varied motivations, the first question they always ask is the same: “Where do I start?” The world of web design and development can be daunting, with its ever-growing list of technologies, tools, and best practices. It can feel like there's a mountain of information to climb, and knowing where to take the first step can be overwhelming. But as with any journey, the key is to simply start somewhere.

This chapter serves as an introduction to the profession of web design before we dive into the technical aspects of building websites. We'll begin by exploring the different roles and responsibilities associated with creating websites, allowing you to consider which path might be right for you. Additionally, I'll provide a comprehensive overview of the equipment and software that are essential to the trade—what you might call the "tools of the trade."

Where Do I Start?

Whether you're reading this book as part of a structured course on web design and development, or you've picked it up to expand your skills on your own, this is a great place to start learning about what makes the web tick. The web design field offers various levels of involvement, from creating a simple personal site to making it a full-fledged career. You might find yourself drawn to becoming a “full-stack” web developer, or perhaps you'll choose to specialize in a particular skill like front-end development, back-end development, or UX design. There are many paths you can take, and the direction you choose will depend on your interests, strengths, and goals.

If you're serious about pursuing web design or production as a career, it's essential to bring your skills up to a professional level. While many employers may not require a formal degree in web design, they will expect to see a portfolio of working sample sites that demonstrate your skills and experience. These sites could be the result of class assignments, personal projects, or even sites you've created for small businesses or organizations. The key is that they should look professional and be backed by well-written, clean HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

For those whose involvement is on a smaller scale—say you just want to publish a site or two—you might find that using a template from an online website service gives you a great head start. Many of these services allow you to tweak the underlying code, so the knowledge you gain from this book will enable you to customize your site to your liking.

It Takes a Village: Website Creation Roles

Creating a website involves more than just writing code and adding pictures. It's a complex process that often begins with a business plan or a clearly defined mission. From there, the content must be created and organized, research needs to be conducted, design must happen—from broad goals to the finest details—code needs to be written, and everything must be coordinated with what's happening on the server to bring the site to fruition.

Big, well-known websites are typically created by large teams of dozens, hundreds, or even thousands of contributors. There are also successful sites maintained by teams with just a handful of members. And in some cases, a single individual manages to create and maintain a respectable site all on their own. That's one of the beauties of the web: its accessibility to creators of all scales.

In this section, I'll introduce you to the various disciplines that contribute to the creation of a site. These include roles related to content, design, and coding. You might end up specializing in just one area, working as part of a team of specialists. Or, if you're designing sites on your own, you might find yourself wearing many hats, much like how maintaining a household requires you to juggle multiple roles—chef, cleaner, accountant, diplomat, and so on. As a solo designer, you'll handle various web-related disciplines, but in time, it will just feel like the stuff you do to make a website.

Content Wrangling

Anyone who takes on the title of “web designer” must understand that everything we do supports the process of getting content, messages, or functionality to our users. Content is king, and without it, even the most beautifully designed websites fall flat. Moreover, good writing can enhance the effectiveness of the user interfaces we create, from button labels to error messages.

Of course, someone needs to create and maintain all that content—and it's important not to underestimate the resources required to do this successfully. Good writers and editors are invaluable members of the team. In addition to general content creation and maintenance, I want to draw your attention to two content-related specialists in modern web development: the Information Architect (IA) and the Content Strategist.

Information Architecture

An Information Architect (also known as an Information Designer) is responsible for organizing the content in a way that is logical and easy to find. This role involves creating search functionality, site diagrams, and organizing the content and data on the server. Information architecture is closely intertwined with UX and UI design, as well as content management. If you enjoy organizing information or are passionate about taxonomies, this might be the job for you. The definitive text for this field, as it relates to the web, is Information Architecture: For the Web and Beyond by Louis Rosenfeld and Peter Morville.

Content Strategy

When the content isn't right, the site can't be fully effective. A Content Strategist ensures that every piece of text on a site—from long-form explanatory text to the labels on buttons—supports the brand identity and marketing goals of the organization. Content strategy may also extend to data modeling and content management on a large, ongoing scale, such as planning for content reuse and update schedules. Their responsibilities may also include managing how the organization's voice is represented on social media. To delve deeper into this field, I recommend the book Content Strategy for the Web, 2nd Edition by Kristina Halvorson and Melissa Rich.

All Manner of Design

When we talk about design in the context of web development, it's easy to think of it as merely how something looks. However, on the web, design is about much more than just aesthetics. Before you even think about colors and fonts, it's crucial to consider how a site works. This involves understanding the site's goals, how it will be used, and how visitors will navigate through it. These tasks fall under the disciplines of User Experience (UX) design, Interaction Design (IxD), and User Interface (UI) design. There's often overlap between these responsibilities, and it's not uncommon for one person or team to handle all three.

User Experience (UX) Design

User Experience design is a holistic approach to design that ensures the entire experience of interacting with the site is favorable. UX design is based on a deep understanding of users and their needs, often gathered through observations and interviews. According to Donald Norman, who coined the term, UX design includes “all aspects of the user's interaction with the product: how it is perceived, learned, and used.” For a website or application, this encompasses everything from visual design to user interface, content quality, and overall site performance. To be successful, the user experience must align with the organization's brand and business goals.

Interaction Design (IxD) and User Interface (UI) Design

The goal of an Interaction Designer is to make the site as easy, efficient, and delightful to use as possible. Closely related is User Interface design, which focuses more narrowly on the functional organization of the page and the specific tools—such as buttons, links, and menus—that users use to navigate content or complete tasks.

The following are common deliverables produced by UX, UI, or interaction designers:

  • User Research and Testing Reports: Understanding the needs, desires, and limitations of users is central to the success of the site or web application. This user-centered design approach often begins with research, including interviews and observations, to better understand how the site can meet its users' needs. The findings might be presented in various formats, such as personas, user stories, or usage scenarios.
  • Wireframes: Wireframes are a blueprint of the site. They show the arrangement of content and interface elements on the pages, as well as the navigational systems. Wireframes help designers organize the site and develop the functionality and content hierarchy. They may also be used as a guide for the visual design team.
  • Prototypes: Prototypes demonstrate how the site or application will function. They range from simple paper sketches to more complex digital mockups that may include basic interactivity. Prototypes are typically used for user testing or to communicate ideas to stakeholders.
  • Usability Testing Reports: As the project progresses, designers often perform usability testing to assess how well the site or application performs in real-world scenarios. This might involve testing how easily users can navigate the site, complete tasks, or find information.

The Visual Designer

At some point in the design process, someone needs to make things look good. That's the role of the Visual Designer, often known as a Graphic Designer or Web Designer. While some people use the term “web design” to refer to any aspect of creating a website, here, I'm using it specifically to describe the process of designing the visual layer of a site. This includes selecting color schemes, fonts, and imagery, as well as creating icons, buttons, and other visual elements.

A good visual designer doesn't just create something that looks appealing—they create something that enhances the site's functionality and aligns with the brand's identity. Good design guides users through the site, making it easier for them to find what they need and complete tasks. It also creates a positive emotional response, making users more likely to return.

To be successful as a visual designer, it's essential to have a good understanding of design principles, such as layout, typography, and color theory. It's also important to have a solid grasp of web-specific considerations, such as designing for different screen sizes and ensuring that designs are accessible to all users.

Wrangling the Code: Frontend, Backend, and Full-Stack Development

The coding aspect of web design can be broken down into three main areas: frontend development, backend development, and full-stack development. While there's often overlap between these roles, it's helpful to understand the distinctions.

Frontend Development

Frontend development, also known as client-side development, involves writing the code that controls how the site appears in the browser and how users interact with it. This includes working with HTML (the structure of the site), CSS (the style of the site), and JavaScript (the interactivity of the site). Frontend developers also need to be familiar with web performance optimization, accessibility, and cross-browser compatibility.

Backend Development

Backend development, also known as server-side development, involves writing the code that powers the server and database. Backend developers work with server-side languages such as PHP, Ruby, Python, and Node.js, as well as database technologies like MySQL, PostgreSQL, and MongoDB. They're responsible for ensuring that the site or application can handle user requests efficiently, store data securely, and scale effectively as the number of users grows.

Full-Stack Development

Full-stack developers are the jack-of-all-trades of web development. They have a solid understanding of both frontend and backend development and can work on all parts of the site or application. While full-stack developers don't necessarily specialize in any one area, their broad knowledge allows them to see the big picture and work on projects from start to finish.

Tools of the Trade

Now that you have an idea of the different roles involved in web design, it's time to look at the tools you'll need to get started. The good news is that you don't need a lot of expensive equipment or software to create a website. Here's a basic list of the tools you'll need:

  • A Computer: You don't need a super-powerful computer to build websites, but you'll want something that's fast enough to run multiple applications at once without slowing down. Most modern laptops and desktops should be up to the task.
  • A Code Editor: You'll need a text editor for writing HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. There are many to choose from, ranging from basic text editors like Notepad++ or Sublime Text to more advanced integrated development environments (IDEs) like Visual Studio Code or WebStorm.
  • A Web Browser: You'll need a web browser to test your sites as you build them. Chrome, Firefox, and Safari are all good choices, and you'll want to test your sites in multiple browsers to ensure cross-browser compatibility.
  • Design Software: If you're doing visual design, you'll need software for creating graphics. Adobe Photoshop and Illustrator are industry standards, but there are also free alternatives like GIMP and Inkscape.
  • Version Control: If you're working on a team or want to keep track of changes to your code, you'll need version control software like Git. GitHub and GitLab are popular platforms for hosting your code repositories.
  • Web Hosting: Finally, you'll need a place to host your site. There are many web hosting services to choose from, ranging from shared hosting to dedicated servers. Some popular choices include Bluehost, SiteGround, and DigitalOcean.
